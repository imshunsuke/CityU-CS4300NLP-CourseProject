\begin{abstract}
We present an end-to-end smart meeting assistant designed for bilingual
(Chinese--English) scenarios common in Hong Kong academic and industrial
settings. The system unifies five participant-facing modules --- automatic
speech recognition with speaker diarization, meeting summarization,
context-aware translation, action-item extraction, and sentiment / engagement
analysis --- over a single shared-transcript representation, together with a
retrieval-augmented Q\&A interface and a live microphone mode for real-time
transcription and translation. Each language-understanding module pairs a
GPT-4o-mini instantiation with a task-specific pretrained baseline
(BART-large-cnn, NLLB-200-distilled-600M), and we introduce a unified
LLM-as-Judge evaluation layer that complements surface-form metrics with
Likert ratings of faithfulness, coverage, and specificity. On AMI, AliMeeting,
MeetingBank, MELD, and news-commentary parallel sentences, the LLM pipeline
matches or exceeds all four specialized baselines while the judge layer
reveals a classical abstractive-vs-extractive trade-off that pure ROUGE and
BLEU obscure.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Meetings in Hong Kong academic, legal, and business settings routinely
switch between Cantonese, Mandarin, and English within a single discussion.
Participants face a compound cognitive load: following the spoken content,
mentally translating across languages, tracking action items, and sensing
the room's emotional dynamic. Existing commercial meeting tools (e.g.\ Zoom
AI Companion, Otter.ai) focus on English and provide post-hoc summaries
rather than in-session support; open-source meeting NLP research has
likewise concentrated on single-language benchmarks such as AMI and
MeetingBank~\cite{hu2023meetingbank}.

We build a smart meeting assistant that explicitly addresses the bilingual
setting and provides both in-session streaming support and post-hoc
structured analysis. Concretely, the system produces six participant-facing
views from a meeting audio input: a speaker-labeled transcript, an
abstractive summary with decisions and follow-ups, a translated transcript
in the complementary language, a table of action items grounded in
transcript spans, a sentiment-and-engagement timeline, and an
embedding-retrieval Q\&A interface. A seventh live mode streams microphone
input through chunked Whisper transcription and asynchronous GPT-4o-mini
translation.

The contributions of this work are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
\item A \textbf{shared-transcript parallel architecture} in which Whisper with
pyannote diarization emits a canonical transcript JSON that four downstream
LLM modules consume independently, enabling parallel evaluation and ablation.
\item \textbf{Bilingual-aware prompting} with a speaker-constrained
JSON-schema function call for action-item extraction, which forces the LLM
to choose assignees from the speaker set produced upstream.
\item A \textbf{unified LLM-as-Judge evaluation layer} that rates every
module's output on faithfulness, coverage, and specificity, complementing
ROUGE / BLEU / F1.
\item A comparative study against four pretrained baselines
(BART-large-cnn, mT5-XLSum, NLLB-200-distilled-600M, sentence-transformers
for retrieval) on AMI, AliMeeting, MeetingBank, and MELD.
\item A \textbf{live streaming mode} combining \texttt{faster-whisper}
chunked ASR with thread-pooled asynchronous translation, demonstrating the
feasibility of real-time participant support on commodity laptops.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
\paragraph{Speech recognition and diarization.}
Whisper~\cite{radford2023whisper} established strong zero-shot ASR across
languages by training on 680k hours of weakly supervised audio.
pyannote.audio~\cite{bredin2020pyannote} remains the standard open-source
diarization stack; WhisperX combines the two with wav2vec2-based forced
alignment to produce word-level timestamps with speaker labels.

\paragraph{Meeting understanding.}
AMI~\cite{radford2023whisper} and ICSI have driven meeting NLP research for
two decades. More recent benchmarks include MeetingBank
\cite{hu2023meetingbank}, a long-form city-council summarization corpus,
and AliMeeting (the M2MeT challenge)~\cite{yu2022m2met}, the canonical
Mandarin multi-party corpus. MELD~\cite{poria2019meld} supplies 7-way
emotion labels on multi-party TV dialogue.

\paragraph{Neural machine translation.}
NLLB-200~\cite{nllb2022} is the de facto open-source baseline for
low-resource translation. Prior comparisons in conversational settings have
noted LLMs' advantage in handling pragmatics and code-switching, which our
numbers corroborate.

\paragraph{LLM-based evaluation.}
LLM-as-a-Judge~\cite{zheng2023judge} scored Likert ratings from GPT-4 to
approximate human judgements on MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena, reporting 80\%
agreement with crowdworkers. We extend the framework to three structured
axes (faithfulness / coverage / specificity) on meeting outputs.
Self-RAG~\cite{asai2024selfrag} and related retrieval-augmented generation
work inform our Q\&A module.

\paragraph{Pretrained summarization baselines.}
BART~\cite{lewis2020bart} and mT5~\cite{xue2021mt5} are the dominant
encoder-decoder pretrained summarizers; both suffer from 1024-token input
limits that prove a decisive disadvantage on meeting-length inputs.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Shared-Transcript Parallel Architecture}

Figure~\ref{fig:arch} shows the pipeline. A single WhisperX pass produces
a canonical \texttt{Transcript} object --- a list of \texttt{Utterance}
records carrying \texttt{(speaker, start, end, text, lang)} --- that all
four downstream modules and the Q\&A index consume without re-running ASR.
This factoring has three payoffs: (i) the ASR/diar cost of roughly
$0.5\times$ real-time on CPU is paid once per audio file (then cached by
SHA-256); (ii) the four downstream modules can be evaluated and ablated
independently; (iii) introducing a new module (here, Q\&A and live mode)
requires no changes to upstream components.

\subsection{Bilingual-Aware Prompting}

The translation module (M3) performs per-utterance ZH$\leftrightarrow$EN
translation with a three-turn context window of prior utterances supplied
as system-visible context, enabling pronoun and antecedent resolution
across speaker turns. A CJK character-ratio heuristic classifies each
utterance's source language when WhisperX returns \texttt{auto}. The
summary module (M2) detects dominant language from the transcript and
emits bullets in that language, so a Mandarin meeting yields a Mandarin
summary without extra configuration.

\subsection{Structured Action-Item Extraction}

M4 uses OpenAI function calling with a strict JSON schema. The
\texttt{assignee} field is declared as an \texttt{enum} populated with the
speaker IDs produced by M1, which makes the model impossible to invent a
non-existent participant as owner. The schema further requires an
\texttt{evidence\_span} --- a direct quote from the transcript --- so every
extracted action is verifiable against its source line:

\begin{small}
\begin{verbatim}
{ "items": [{
    "task": str, "assignee": one_of(speakers),
    "due": str|null, "priority": "H"|"M"|"L",
    "evidence_span": str }] }
\end{verbatim}
\end{small}

\subsection{Live Streaming Mode}

The live tab captures microphone audio via Gradio's streaming callback
(delta chunks, typically 500\,ms each). Our \texttt{MicStream} class
accumulates a 1-second buffer, flushes via \texttt{faster-whisper base}
(int8, CPU), and dispatches each resulting utterance to a thread-pool
worker that performs the translation LLM call. The transcript therefore
appears in the UI within roughly 1--2\,s of the end of the spoken
utterance, while the translation catches up one or two stream events
later. A user-facing \textbf{Process now} button force-flushes residual
audio when the speaker pauses below the buffer threshold.

\subsection{Unified LLM-as-Judge Evaluation}

ROUGE and BLEU are n-gram surface-form metrics and BART's 1024-token
truncation is known to depress them on meeting-length inputs. We
therefore run a secondary evaluation pass where GPT-4o (the larger sibling
of our system's GPT-4o-mini) rates every \{transcript, output, system\}
triple on three 1--5 Likert axes: \emph{faithfulness} (does the output
avoid hallucination?), \emph{coverage} (is important content captured?),
and \emph{specificity} (is the output concrete rather than vague?). We
explicitly avoid using GPT-4o-mini as the judge to prevent self-preference
bias~\cite{zheng2023judge}. This layer lets us diagnose the qualitative
pattern that surface metrics hide in §\ref{sec:exp} and §\ref{sec:disc}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:disc}

\paragraph{BART context bottleneck dominates long meetings.}
The ROUGE-1 gap between GPT-4o-mini and BART widens from +0.066 on a
2-minute clip to +0.230 on the full 30-minute meeting
(Figure~\ref{fig:rouge}). BART's 1024-token truncation forces it to see
only the first few minutes of a meeting, at which point our short-clip
numbers become its ceiling. This demonstrates that long-context LLMs are
not merely convenient but structurally necessary for meeting-length inputs.

\paragraph{Surface metrics miss the faithfulness story.}
Both ROUGE-1 and ROUGE-L nominally favor GPT, yet the LLM-as-Judge reveals
that BART is actually \emph{more faithful} on its truncated input; GPT's
advantage is in covering more of the meeting and producing more concrete
phrasing. A summary system optimized purely for ROUGE would miss this
behavior. This validates our decision to include the judge layer as a
methodological novelty rather than relying on any single metric.

\paragraph{WER as a misleading quality proxy.}
Our 0.407 WER on AMI is markedly worse than Whisper's reported ${\sim}0.15$
on clean read speech. On inspection, much of the ``error'' is Whisper's
discourse normalization: ``she gonna answer me'' in the AMI literal
transcript becomes ``Is she going to answer me?'' in Whisper output. For
downstream understanding (M2--M5) this is arguably an improvement; WER
penalizes it as substitutions. Text-normalization-aware WER would be more
informative.

\paragraph{Live-mode latency budget.}
Our live mode's end-to-end latency from mouth-closed to on-screen
translation is roughly 2--4\,s on an Apple-Silicon MacBook, dominated by
the 1-second audio buffer and a 1--2\,s \texttt{faster-whisper base} CPU
pass. Translation is asynchronous and catches up within one further stream
event. This is sufficient for ``near-real-time'' participant support (e.g.\
following a meeting in a second language) but not yet for truly live
simultaneous interpretation, which would require on-GPU streaming ASR or
an OpenAI Whisper API backend.

\paragraph{Small-N caveat.}
Many of our numbers (WER, ROUGE, actions F1, LLM-judge) currently come
from a single AMI meeting because pipeline validation took precedence over
scale. We describe the infrastructure that scales the evaluation to
dozens of meetings (\texttt{eval/data\_prep/ami.py}) and leave the
larger-N study to future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We built and evaluated a bilingual smart meeting assistant organized
around a shared-transcript parallel architecture, with four GPT-4o-mini
modules, a retrieval-augmented Q\&A interface, and a live streaming mode.
GPT-4o-mini matches or exceeds specialized pretrained baselines
(BART, mT5, NLLB-200) on meeting summarization and translation, and
our LLM-as-Judge layer surfaces a faithfulness / coverage trade-off that
surface metrics hide. Future work should (i) scale evaluation to the full
AMI and AliMeeting partitions, (ii) swap the live ASR backend to an
OpenAI Whisper API or mlx-whisper for GPU-accelerated streaming, and
(iii) extend the judge rubric to incorporate bilingual code-switching
adequacy.
