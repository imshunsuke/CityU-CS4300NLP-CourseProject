\title{Smart Meeting Assistant: A Shared-Transcript Modular Pipeline with \\
an LLM-as-Judge Pilot for Bilingual Chinese--English Meetings}
\author{CS4300 NLP Group Project (Topic 5) \\
Department of Computer Science, City University of Hong Kong}
\date{April 2026}

\begin{abstract}
We present an end-to-end smart meeting assistant for bilingual
(Chinese--English) settings that unifies five participant-facing
modules---ASR with speaker diarization, summarization, translation,
action-item extraction, and sentiment / engagement analysis---over
a single shared-transcript representation, with a retrieval-augmented
Q\&A interface and a live-microphone mode. Each language-understanding
module pairs a GPT-4o-mini instantiation with a task-specific
pretrained baseline (BART-large-CNN, NLLB-200 distilled 600\,M), and
we run a small LLM-as-Judge pilot to complement ROUGE/BLEU with
Likert ratings of faithfulness, coverage, and specificity. On AMI
ES2004a, AliMeeting R8001\_M8004, MELD, and 26 news-commentary
EN$\leftrightarrow$ZH pairs, the LLM matches or exceeds the
specialized baselines on surface metrics; the judge pilot surfaces a
faithfulness-vs-coverage trade-off between the long-context LLM and
the 1\,024-token BART that ROUGE alone hides. We report honest
per-direction translation numbers and a task-detection-only
action-item evaluation, and discuss the system's current limits.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Meetings in Hong Kong academic, legal, and business settings routinely
switch between Cantonese, Mandarin, and English. Participants face a
compound cognitive load: following the spoken content, mentally
translating across languages, tracking action items, and sensing the
room's emotional dynamic. Commercial tools (Zoom AI Companion, Otter.ai)
focus on English and provide post-hoc summaries rather than in-session
support, and most open-source meeting NLP research has concentrated on
single-language benchmarks such as AMI~\cite{carletta2006ami}.

We build a smart meeting assistant that explicitly addresses the
bilingual setting and provides both in-session streaming support and
post-hoc structured analysis. Given a meeting audio input the system
produces six participant-facing views: a speaker-labeled transcript, an
abstractive summary with decisions and follow-ups, a translated
transcript in the complementary language, a table of action items
grounded in transcript spans, a sentiment-and-engagement timeline, and
an embedding-retrieval Q\&A interface. A seventh live mode streams
microphone input through chunked Whisper transcription and asynchronous
GPT-4o-mini translation.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item A \textbf{shared-transcript modular architecture} in which
WhisperX with pyannote diarization emits a canonical transcript JSON
that four downstream LLM modules consume independently, enabling
independent evaluation and ablation.
\item \textbf{Bilingual-aware prompting}: WhisperX emits one
meeting-level language tag, and a CJK character-ratio heuristic is
used as a per-utterance fallback only when that tag is
\texttt{auto}; on top of that we apply a speaker-constrained
function-calling schema for action-item extraction that forces the
LLM to choose assignees from the upstream speaker set.
\item An \textbf{LLM-as-Judge pilot} (three triples, summarization and
actions) that complements ROUGE/BLEU with Likert ratings on
faithfulness, coverage, and specificity.
\item A \textbf{live streaming mode} that combines \texttt{faster-%
whisper base} chunked ASR with a thread-pool of asynchronous
translation LLM calls on commodity laptops.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
\paragraph{Speech recognition and diarization.}
Whisper~\cite{radford2023whisper} established strong zero-shot ASR
via 680\,k~h of weakly supervised training.
pyannote.audio~\cite{bredin2020pyannote} with the powerset
formulation~\cite{plaquet23powerset} remains the standard open-source
diarization stack; WhisperX combines the two with wav2vec2 forced
alignment. We use these as unmodified building blocks.

\paragraph{Meeting corpora and baselines.}
AMI~\cite{carletta2006ami} and AliMeeting (M2MeT)~\cite{yu2022m2met}
are the canonical English and Mandarin multi-party meeting corpora;
MELD~\cite{poria2019meld} supplies 7-way conversational emotion
labels. On the generation side, BART~\cite{lewis2020bart} is the
default encoder-decoder summarizer (1\,024-token limit, decisive on
meeting-length inputs), and NLLB-200~\cite{nllb2022} is the de
facto MT baseline---its distilled 600\,M checkpoint performs
markedly worse on EN$\to$ZH than on ZH$\to$EN in our benchmark
(Sec.~\ref{sec:exp}).

\paragraph{LLM-based evaluation.}
Zheng~\emph{et~al.}~\cite{zheng2023judge} score Likert ratings from
GPT-4 to approximate human judgements, reporting ${\sim}80\%$
agreement with crowdworkers. We adopt their framework on
faithfulness / coverage / specificity and use GPT-4o (not our
system's GPT-4o-mini) as the judge to limit self-preference bias.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Shared-Transcript Modular Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}

Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} shows the pipeline. A single WhisperX pass emits
a canonical \texttt{Transcript} JSON object---a list of
\texttt{Utterance} records carrying \texttt{(speaker,$t_s$,$t_e$,%
text,lang)}---that all four downstream modules and the Q\&A index
consume without re-running ASR. This factoring has three payoffs:
(i)~the ASR and diarization cost of roughly $0.5\times$ real-time on
CPU is paid once per audio file (then cached by SHA-256); (ii)~the
downstream modules can be evaluated and ablated independently; and
(iii)~introducing a new module (here, Q\&A and live mode) requires no
changes to upstream components. The four language-understanding
modules are independent in the DAG sense but are currently executed
serially; the architecture does not prevent a parallel driver.

\subsection{Bilingual-Aware Prompting}

The translation module (M3) performs per-utterance ZH$\leftrightarrow$EN
translation with a three-turn sliding context window of prior
utterances, enabling pronoun and antecedent resolution across speaker
turns. Language handling is \emph{meeting-level} by default:
WhisperX stamps one language tag on every utterance, and our
CJK character-ratio heuristic only fires as a fallback when that tag
is \texttt{auto}. This is adequate for the monolingual AMI and
AliMeeting meetings we evaluate, but a proper per-utterance LID
(e.g., VoxLingua) would be needed for real code-switched Hong~Kong
meetings; we leave that to future work. The summary module (M2)
detects the dominant language from the transcript and emits bullets
in that language, so a Mandarin meeting yields a Mandarin summary
without extra configuration.

\subsection{Structured Action-Item Extraction}

M4 uses OpenAI function calling with a strict JSON schema. The
\texttt{assignee} field is declared as an \texttt{enum} populated with
the speaker IDs produced by M1, so the model cannot invent a
non-existent participant as owner. The schema further requires an
\texttt{evidence\_span}---a direct quote from the transcript---so every
extracted action is verifiable against its source line:
{\small
\begin{verbatim}
{ "items": [{
    "task": str, "assignee": one_of(speakers),
    "due": str|null, "priority": "H"|"M"|"L",
    "evidence_span": str }] }
\end{verbatim}}

\subsection{Live Streaming Mode}

The live tab captures microphone audio via Gradio's streaming
callback (500\,ms delta chunks). Our \texttt{MicStream} class
accumulates a 1-second buffer, runs \texttt{faster-whisper base}
(int8, CPU) on the buffer, and dispatches each resulting utterance to
a thread-pool worker for the translation LLM call. Live mode does
\emph{not} run pyannote diarization---it is too heavy for 1-s chunks
on CPU---so utterances are emitted under a single \texttt{SPEAKER}
label; the batch pipeline retains full speaker attribution.
A \textbf{Process now} button force-flushes residual audio when the
speaker pauses below the buffer threshold.

\subsection{LLM-as-Judge Pilot}
\label{sec:judge}

ROUGE and BLEU are n-gram surface-form metrics, and BART's
1\,024-token truncation is known to depress them on meeting-length
inputs. We therefore run a small LLM-as-Judge pilot (3 triples:
GPT summary, BART summary, and GPT action-item list---all on the AMI
ES2004a full meeting) where GPT-4o rates each \{transcript, output,
system\} triple on three 1--5 Likert axes: \emph{faithfulness}
(does the output avoid hallucination?), \emph{coverage}
(is important content captured?), and \emph{specificity}
(is the output concrete rather than vague?). We cap the transcript
shown to the judge at 8\,000 characters to fit its context budget.
This is a pilot, not a full human-correlation study, and its sample
size is explicitly small; Sec.~\ref{sec:disc} explains how the signal
still complements surface metrics.

\subsection{Module 1 --- ASR with Diarization}
\label{sec:m1}
We report Word Error Rate for English (AMI ES2004a) and Character

lowercase-and-punctuation-stripped normalization applied identically
to hypothesis and reference (Table~\ref{tab:asr}). For diarization we
use pyannote-3.1 unmodified; the underlying diarization formulation
is described in~\cite{plaquet23powerset}. We do \emph{not} report
Diarization Error Rate in this paper---our pipeline change is
limited to the integration of that third-party model into WhisperX,
and scoring DER against AMI's RTTM labels is left to future work.
The elevated WER reflects Whisper's discourse normalization

\subsection{Module 2 --- Summarization}
We compare GPT-4o-mini against BART-large-CNN on two regimes: the
first two minutes of ES2004a (short-clip ablation) and the full
30-minute meeting. ROUGE is computed against the AMI reference
abstract (Fig.~\ref{fig:rouge}). GPT's 128\,K-token context handles
the full meeting; BART's 1\,024-token limit forces truncation, so its
full-meeting ROUGE-1 actually drops below its own short-clip ceiling
while GPT's rises. The ROUGE-1 gap widens from $+0.066$ on the
clip to $+0.230$ on the full meeting.

\subsection{Module 3 --- Translation}
We sample 13~EN--ZH pairs from
\texttt{Helsinki-NLP/news\_commentary}~ and evaluate in both
directions (26 hypotheses total), each direction scored with its
sacreBLEU-appropriate tokenizer (\texttt{zh} for ZH targets,
\texttt{13a} for EN targets) following~\cite{post2018sacrebleu}. Our
batch pipeline translates each utterance with a three-turn context
window; the BLEU benchmark translates isolated sentences
(i.e., no context) and is therefore a conservative proxy for the
system's full in-meeting quality. Fig.~\ref{fig:bleu} reports the
per-direction scores.

\subsection{Module 4 --- Action-Item Detection}
AMI's abstractive summary annotations include a machine-readable
\texttt{<actions>} section. We score our function-calling output
against those gold actions using greedy cosine-similarity matching
over sentence-transformer embeddings (threshold 0.55).
ES2004a annotates one gold action and no gold assignees; our system
extracts four items, the one gold task plus three procedural routine
items (Table~\ref{tab:actions}). This evaluation therefore measures
\emph{task detection only}. Structured fields (\texttt{due},
\texttt{priority}, and \texttt{evidence\_span}) are produced and
human-inspectable in the UI but are not scored against gold here,
because AMI does not annotate them.

\subsection{Module 5 --- Sentiment / Emotion}
Per-utterance 7-way emotion classification on 150 randomly sampled
MELD \emph{dev} utterances yields
\textbf{0.580~accuracy / 0.519~macro-F1}. This is a zero-shot result
with no task-specific fine-tuning and no dialogue context. Supervised
context-aware systems on MELD typically reach 0.65--0.70 accuracy; our
number is competitive for the zero-shot, single-utterance setting we
evaluate, and we do not yet test on Chinese meetings---a clear next
step. Fig.~\ref{fig:st-judge}(a) visualises the valence timeline
produced by the module on ES2004a.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:disc}

\paragraph{BART context bottleneck dominates long meetings.}
The ROUGE-1 gap between GPT-4o-mini and BART widens from $+0.066$
on a 2-minute clip to $+0.230$ on the full 30-minute meeting
(Fig.~\ref{fig:rouge}). BART's 1\,024-token truncation forces it to
see only the first few minutes of the meeting, so our short-clip
numbers effectively cap its full-meeting ceiling. Long-context LLMs
are not just convenient here---they are structurally necessary.

\paragraph{Direction asymmetry in translation.}
The per-direction BLEU reveals a story a single corpus-BLEU would
flatten. On our 13 EN$\to$ZH pairs, GPT-4o-mini scores 30.01 against
NLLB's 8.08; on the 13 ZH$\to$EN pairs the two systems score 23.68
and 23.42 respectively, a 0.26 BLEU gap that we do \emph{not}
interpret as meaningful on so few pairs and without resampling-based
intervals. The EN$\to$ZH asymmetry is consistent with the known
weakness of distilled NLLB on generate-Chinese directions. BLEU does
not aggregate linearly across target languages, so we report the two
directions side-by-side and avoid a single summary score.

\paragraph{Surface metrics miss faithfulness.}
Both ROUGE-1 and ROUGE-L nominally favour GPT, yet the judge pilot
(Fig.~\ref{fig:st-judge}(b)) finds BART \emph{more faithful} on the
truncated input it sees; GPT's advantage is in covering more of the
meeting and producing more concrete phrasing. A summary system
optimized purely for ROUGE would not surface this behaviour. The
pilot is small (three triples) and we therefore frame it as a
qualitative diagnostic rather than a claim of statistical superiority.

\paragraph{WER as a misleading quality proxy.}
Our 0.407 WER on AMI is markedly worse than Whisper's reported
${\sim}0.15$ on clean read speech, but much of the gap is discourse
normalization (``she gonna answer me'' in the AMI literal transcript
becomes ``Is she going to answer me?'' in Whisper output). For
downstream understanding (M2--M5) this is arguably an improvement;
normalization-aware WER would be more informative.

\paragraph{Live-mode latency budget.}
Manual stopwatch observation on a single Apple-Silicon MacBook places
end-to-end latency from mouth-closed to on-screen translation at
roughly 2--4\,s, dominated by the 1-second audio buffer and a 1--2\,s
\texttt{faster-whisper base} CPU pass (translation is asynchronous).
This is an eyeballed estimate, not an instrumented measurement; it is
sufficient for ``near-real-time'' participant support but not truly
simultaneous interpretation, which would need GPU-streaming ASR or a
server-side Whisper API. Adding a latency logger is on our short-term
to-do list.

\paragraph{Scope and small-$N$ caveats.}
Most numbers come from a single AMI meeting, and the judge pilot
scores only three triples. The data-prep scripts in
\texttt{eval/data\_prep/*.py} already scale to dozens of meetings,
and Chinese-meeting evaluation beyond a 2-minute CER spot check is
left to future work. We do not score DER: pyannote-3.1 is used
unmodified~\cite{plaquet23powerset} and scoring it against AMI's
RTTM labels is left to future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We built a bilingual smart meeting assistant organised around a
shared-transcript modular architecture, with four GPT-4o-mini modules,
retrieval-augmented Q\&A, and a live streaming mode. GPT-4o-mini
matches or exceeds BART and NLLB-200 on meeting summarization and
EN$\to$ZH translation, and the LLM-as-Judge pilot surfaces a
faithfulness / coverage trade-off that surface metrics hide.
Future work: (i)~scale to the full AMI/AliMeeting partitions and
score DER; (ii)~swap the live ASR backend to GPU-streaming or a
server Whisper API; (iii)~replace the CJK-ratio heuristic with a
proper per-utterance LID for code-switched meetings; and
(iv)~expand the judge study to include human correlation.
